Three changes, one set of directions

The amendment uplifts most reimbursable rates by ~3.5%, introduces a brand-new GP Reimbursement Scheme as a successor pot for CAIP-funded GP roles, and shifts one childhood immunisation from opportunistic to call/recall.

Headline change

GP Reimbursement Scheme

A new ring-fenced pot to fund new salaried GPs, increase sessions for existing salaried GPs, or continue posts previously funded under Capacity & Access Payments and the PCN Test Sites Programme.

The GP Reimbursement Scheme

Section 14A of the Principal Directions. A new payment scheme for the financial year ending 31 March 2027, with three claim routes and a hard cap of £4.57 × adjusted practice population.

Annual scheme cap
£4.57
per adjusted patient
per practice, per year
Three principles shape the cap and how it can be used:
  • Additionality — cannot duplicate another reimbursement, cannot cover absent GPs
  • Practices over 3,500 patients per GP need NHSE pre-approval to participate
  • Entitlement may be transferred between Core Network Members of the same PCN
Route A

New salaried GP

Employing a salaried GP not employed by the contractor in the past 12 months (retirement returnees and leave-cover GPs are excepted). Must meet additionality.

Up to £152,900 / £155,698 London
Route B

Increased sessions

Lifting an existing salaried GP from below 9 sessions per week up to a maximum of 9 sessions. Hourly cap applies; covers salary, ER NI and pension.

Up to £90.61/hr · £92.27 London
Route C

Continuation of CAIP / Test Sites GPs

Where a post was previously funded by Capacity & Access Payments or the PCN Test Sites Programme and that funding has ended or is ending. PCN approval required.

Up to £152,900 / £155,698 London
Claims process — all claims via CQRSL, submitted within 3 months of the end of the month they relate to. Paid in arrears at the end of the following calendar month. Practices fund the wage-cycle gap.

Issues to watch

Eight practical considerations as you put the scheme into operation. The financial cap looks generous on paper but interacts with eligibility rules and CAIP transition in ways that need careful planning.

01

Real-terms cut for CAIP-maxed practices

Where CAIP funded GP posts at higher levels, the £4.57 cap is materially tighter. Route C provides continuity but does not restore the previous envelope. Model against actual CAIP draw-down per practice.

02

The 12-month additionality rule

Practices cannot rehire a salaried GP let go in the past year — except retirement returnees and leave-cover GPs. Recent restructures may put returning GPs out of scope.

03

3,500 patients per GP gate

NHS England approval required to participate where the ratio is above 3,500. Most practices likely sit above this. Coordinate one PCN-level submission rather than multiple separate ones.

04

PCN-level transfer needs governance

Paragraph (11) allows transfer of entitlement between Core Network Members. Useful for PCN workforce planning, but practices need a Standing Order or Programme Board mechanism to allocate fairly.

05

9-session ceiling on Route B

Increased participation tops out at 9 sessions per week — essentially full-time. Limits creative use to incentivise portfolio GPs above that threshold.

06

Working capital impact

Paid in arrears at the end of the calendar month after submission. Practices fund the wage-cycle gap. Worth flagging in PCN finance discussions for smaller members.

07

CQRSL admin burden

Monthly claim cycles plus a declaration against each route's principles per claim. Strong candidate for automated declaration templates and a tracker.

08

Immunisation workload uplift

The 3y4m vaccine moving to call/recall means active outreach. Plan into call-and-recall lists, admin time, and QOF/IIF performance trajectories.
